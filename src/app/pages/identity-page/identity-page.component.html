Identity
The flower is made of non-flower elements. We can describe the flower as being full of everything. There is nothing that is not present in the flower. We see sunshine, we see the rain, we see clouds, we see the earth, and we also see time and space in the flower. A flower, like everything else, is made entirely of non-flower elements. The whole cosmos has come together in order to help the flower manifest herself, The flower is full of everything except one thing: a separate self, a separate identity. The flower cannot be by herself alone. The flower has to inter-be with the sunshine, the cloud and everything in the cosmos. If we understand being in terms of inter-being, then we are much closer to the truth. Inter-being is not being and it is not non-being. Inter-being means being empty of a separate identity, empty of a separate self.

What is an identity?
We use the concept of an identity to distinguish ourselves as individuals within a group. Our identity is composed of a unique set of pieces of information, for example: name, date of birth, location of birth, etc. This information alone almost nearly uniquely distinguishes every person in the world. But this is only information about us as individuals.

From the sociological perspective, our identity is composed of our relationships to other individuals. A group of people who know one another could be described as a social network.

This could perhaps be visualized as something not so different from the ball-and-stick model that is used in chemistry. In chemistry, perhaps any one hydrogen atom is virtually identical to any other hydrogen atom. But with respect to some kind of molecule as an entity, for example C12H17N2O4P , there might be 17 such individual hydrogen atoms, but their individuality is moot. What is of importance is not just the atoms themselves, but the arrangement of such atoms, in order to create the molecule. In a similar way, in the entity of the social network, a person's identity is not exclusively their individuality (the information which is unique to them as individuals), rather, it is the arrangement of the relationships which distinguishes each member with what we can refer to as their identity.

In the biological-sociological world, this business of knowing the identity of other people happens automatically without effort. Biology has equipped us with a brain that is designed to efficiently process identity information about other people. Facial recognition, voice recognition, and a variety of other subtle and obvious sensory inputs are quickly processed to authenticate the person that we are meeting; that is to say that our brains take the sensory information about a person and matches it to the existing conscious and subconscious knowledge that we have about a person, thereby identifying them. This biological functionality is generally robust and reliable, and is an important part of the foundation upon which ODIN is based.


An accounting of identities as a single congruous network entity
We can therefore create a system that takes into account this kind of sociological relationship information to use as knowledge of identities of the users. The input of this data becomes the base dataset for network identity authentication. This input of data into the network is a crowd-sourced operation. The software assists the users in having them input as much social relationship information as they have knowledge of. In essence, the users enter in what is essentially address-book contact information about all of the people that they know in real life. This contact list, created by the user, acts as the functional contact list for the user. As more users join the network, mutually known information becomes repeatedly corroborated. After some reasonable threshold, the information becomes considered reliably accurate.

This data is stored on the nodes and accessed by the software on the nodes.
